What vitamin C actually does

Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) is an essential water-soluble vitamin — your body cannot produce it, so you must obtain it from food or supplementation daily. Its functions are wide-ranging and well-documented across thousands of clinical studies.

Evidence-backed functions

Immune support: Stimulates production and function of white blood cells, including neutrophils and lymphocytes. Collagen synthesis: Vitamin C is required for collagen production — without it, skin, joints, bones, and connective tissue cannot be built or repaired properly. Antioxidant protection: Neutralizes free radicals and regenerates other antioxidants like vitamin E. Iron absorption: Taken with plant-based iron, vitamin C dramatically increases absorption. Wound healing: Required at every stage of wound repair. Stress response: The adrenal glands contain the highest concentration of vitamin C in the body and use it intensively during stress.

Who's actually deficient?

Severe deficiency (scurvy) is rare in the developed world. But subclinical insufficiency is common — particularly among smokers (who need 2x the standard dose), people with high stress, poor diet quality, or chronic illness. Subclinical deficiency shows as impaired wound healing, frequent colds, fatigue, and skin that bruises easily.

Vitamin C is also depleted by common medications including aspirin, oral contraceptives, and some antacids. If you're regularly taking any of these, maintaining adequate vitamin C intake becomes more important.

Honest perspective

For people eating a diet with adequate fruits and vegetables, high-dose vitamin C supplementation adds limited benefit. The biggest gains come from correcting insufficiency — ensuring you're consistently meeting your daily requirement, especially during illness, high stress, or periods of intense exercise.

Forms of vitamin C — what to choose

Ascorbic Acid
Standard, effective

Pure vitamin C. Highly effective and well-absorbed. Can cause GI upset at high doses on empty stomach. The most studied form — this is what's in most trials.

Buffered (Calcium Ascorbate)
Gentle on stomach

Neutralized with calcium, making it non-acidic. Ideal for people with sensitive stomachs or GERD. Slightly lower in pure vitamin C per mg but better tolerated at higher doses.

With Bioflavonoids
Enhanced absorption

Citrus bioflavonoids and rose hips help optimize vitamin C utilization in the body. Recommended over plain ascorbic acid for daily supplementation.

Liposomal Vitamin C
High dose protocols

Encapsulated in lipid carriers for superior absorption. Best for therapeutic high-dose protocols. More expensive — not necessary for everyday maintenance supplementation.

Dosage guide

Vitamin C dosage by goal
Daily maintenance
500-1000mg per day is a common and well-supported dose for general health maintenance. The RDA is only 75-90mg (women/men), but this is the minimum to prevent deficiency, not the optimal level.
Daily
Immune support
1000-2000mg per day during illness or cold season. Split into 2 doses to maintain plasma levels throughout the day. Higher single doses are mostly excreted rather than absorbed.
Active illness
Smokers
Add at least 35mg above standard recommendations (per NIH guidelines). Practical target: 1000mg daily. Smoking significantly depletes vitamin C through oxidative stress.
Daily
Collagen support
250-500mg taken together with collagen peptides significantly enhances collagen synthesis. Timing with collagen supplements is more important than total daily dose here.
With collagen
Upper limit
The tolerable upper limit is 2000mg/day. Above this, GI side effects (loose stools, cramps) become likely. Kidney stone risk increases at very high long-term doses in susceptible individuals.

Frequently asked questions

Does vitamin C actually prevent colds?
The research is nuanced. Regular supplementation does not prevent colds in the general population, but it does reduce cold duration by about 8-14% in adults, and shows stronger effects in people under intense physical stress (athletes, soldiers). Starting vitamin C after a cold begins has less evidence.
Can I take too much vitamin C?
The tolerable upper limit is 2000mg per day. Above this, diarrhea and GI cramps become likely. Long-term very high doses may increase kidney stone risk in susceptible individuals. 500-1000mg daily is well within safe limits for most people.
Is vitamin C better taken with food or on an empty stomach?
With food is generally better tolerated, especially at higher doses. Vitamin C's acidity can cause stomach irritation on an empty stomach. If you experience GI upset, switch to a buffered form (calcium ascorbate).
Does vitamin C interfere with any supplements?
Vitamin C enhances iron absorption — take together with iron-rich foods or iron supplements if you want to maximize absorption. Avoid taking high-dose vitamin C simultaneously with B12 supplements as it may reduce B12 absorption slightly.
Should I take vitamin C with collagen?
Yes — vitamin C is required for collagen synthesis. Taking 250-500mg with your collagen supplement is the recommended approach. The combination is more effective than either alone.
